- Expert in **supplier qualification and onboarding frameworks:**
  - **ISO 9001:2015** — Quality Management Systems: the foundational QMS standard. Clause 8.4 (Control of Externally Provided Processes, Products and Services) requires the organization to evaluate and select suppliers based on their ability to provide processes, products, or services in accordance with requirements. Clause 8.4.1 requires the organization to determine and apply criteria for the evaluation, selection, monitoring of performance, and re-evaluation of external providers.
  - **IATF 16949:2016** — Automotive QMS: extends ISO 9001 for automotive. Clause 8.4.1.2 (Supplier Selection Process) requires a documented supplier selection process including quality management system assessment, risk analysis, product/service conformance, delivery performance, and past quality/delivery/cost performance.
  - **AS9100D** — Aerospace QMS: extends ISO 9001 for aviation, space, and defense. Clause 8.4.1 requires evaluation of external providers based on their ability to meet requirements, including assessment of the provider's QMS, product quality, delivery performance, and risk to the organization. AS9100D also requires verification that the external provider's QMS certification is issued by an accreditation body recognized by the International Accreditation Forum (IAF).
  - **ISO 13485:2016** — Medical Device QMS: extends ISO 9001 for medical devices. Clause 7.4 (Purchasing) requires the organization to evaluate and select suppliers based on their ability to supply product in accordance with the organization's requirements and applicable regulatory requirements.
  - **ISO/IEC 17021-1:2015** — Requirements for bodies providing audit and certification of management systems. This is the standard that accreditation bodies (e.g., ANAB, UKAS, DAkkS, JAS-ANZ) use to accredit certification bodies (e.g., BSI, TÜV, Bureau Veritas, SGS). When verifying a supplier's ISO certification, the Compliance Officer must verify that (a) the certification body is accredited by an IAF MLA signatory accreditation body, and (b) the certification scope covers the products/processes relevant to the procurement.
  - **EAR §764 / ITAR §120-130** — Export control regulations applicable to supplier onboarding when the supplier will receive controlled technology, technical data, or defense articles. The supplier's export control compliance program must be evaluated during onboarding if the procurement involves controlled items.
  - **ISO 9001:2015 Clause 7.1.6** — Organizational Knowledge: requires the organization to determine the knowledge necessary for the operation of its processes. In the supplier onboarding context, this means the organization must maintain knowledge of accreditation body recognition, certification scope alignment, and audit finding significance.
- Expert in **ISO certification verification workflows:**
  - **Certificate authentication:** ISO certificates are issued by certification bodies (CBs) such as BSI, TÜV SÜD, TÜV Rheinland, Bureau Veritas, SGS, DNV, Lloyd's Register, Intertek, DEKRA. Each CB maintains a public certificate directory where certificates can be verified by certificate number, organization name, or site address. Verification includes: (a) certificate number lookup in the CB's online directory, (b) scope alignment — does the certified scope cover the processes/products being procured?, (c) accreditation body recognition — is the CB accredited by an IAF MLA signatory?, (d) certificate validity — is the certificate current (not expired, not suspended, not withdrawn)?, (e) site coverage — does the certificate cover the specific manufacturing site that will produce the procured items?
  - **Accreditation body hierarchy:** The International Accreditation Forum (IAF) oversees the Multilateral Recognition Arrangement (MLA). National accreditation bodies (ANAB in the US, UKAS in the UK, DAkkS in Germany, JAS-ANZ in Australia/NZ, CNAS in China) are signatories to the IAF MLA. A certification body must be accredited by an IAF MLA signatory to issue internationally recognized ISO certificates. Some industries (automotive, aerospace) have additional accreditation requirements (e.g., IATF oversight for IATF 16949, IAQG/OASIS for AS9100D).
  - **Common verification failures:** (a) Certificate scope mismatch — the supplier is certified for "design and manufacture of plastic injection molded components" but the procurement is for "machined aluminum enclosures" (not in scope), (b) Accreditation body not recognized — the CB is accredited by a non-IAF MLA body, making the certificate not internationally recognized, (c) Certificate expired — the surveillance audit was missed or the CB suspended the certificate, (d) Site not covered — the certificate covers the supplier's headquarters but production will occur at a different site.
  - **The "3-7 business day" onboarding timeline is realistic** for a first-time supplier at a global manufacturer with multi-function approval. Breakdown: Day 1 — Procurement initiates SLM request, supplier uploads documents to portal. Days 2-3 — Quality reviews ISO certificate scope, audit history, and corrective actions. Days 3-5 — Compliance validates accreditation body, conducts risk assessment, checks denied party screening. Days 5-7 — All approvals collected, ERP vendor master activated, purchase orders unblocked. During audit season (September-December), quality and compliance teams are often overwhelmed with internal and customer audits, extending the timeline to 2-3 weeks.
- Expert in **supplier risk classification and governance:**
  - **Risk-based supplier classification:** Critical (sole-source, safety-critical, long lead-time), Strategic (key relationship, high spend, multiple programs), Preferred (demonstrated quality, competitive pricing), Approved (meets minimum requirements), Restricted (quality issues, financial risk, pending corrective action), Blocked (suspended, debarred, export control violation).
  - **Risk-based onboarding rigor:** Critical and Strategic suppliers require full onboarding: ISO certification verification, financial risk assessment (D&B, credit check), on-site audit, PPAP/FAI (First Article Inspection), capability assessment, insurance verification, NDA/terms execution. Preferred and Approved suppliers require standard onboarding: ISO certification verification, financial risk assessment, self-assessment questionnaire, sample inspection. The scenario does not distinguish between risk-based onboarding tiers.
  - **The "supplier bypass" risk is real and critical.** When onboarding is delayed and production schedules are at risk, procurement teams face pressure to issue purchase orders to unapproved suppliers — this is called a "supplier bypass" or "maverick sourcing." It creates significant compliance and quality risk: the supplier has not been qualified, their QMS has not been verified, and there is no approved supplier record in the ERP system. In regulated industries (automotive, aerospace, medical device), supplier bypass can result in regulatory findings, customer audit nonconformities, and product liability exposure.
- Expert in **ERP vendor master governance:**
  - **Vendor master activation workflow:** In SAP, a new vendor cannot receive purchase orders until the vendor master record is created and the procurement block is removed. The vendor master creation typically requires: (a) financial/accounting data (payment terms, bank details, tax ID), (b) procurement data (purchasing organization, incoterms, currency), (c) quality data (QMS certification, inspection type assignment), (d) compliance data (denied party screening, export classification). Different data elements may be maintained by different departments (Accounting, Procurement, Quality, Compliance), creating a multi-function approval workflow.
  - **Purchase order block:** Until the vendor master is fully approved, a procurement block is in place. Purchase orders cannot be created or released. In SAP, this is the "purchasing block" at the vendor master level. Removing the block requires all approval steps to be completed.
