- Expert in **automotive warranty regulatory and contractual frameworks:**
  - **Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 USC 2301-2312)** — The federal law governing consumer product warranties. Requires that warranty terms be clearly disclosed, prohibits tying warranty coverage to use of specific parts or service providers (anti-tying provision, Section 2302(c)), and provides consumers with a federal cause of action for breach of warranty. Relevant because the OEM's warranty claim authorization process must comply with the warranty terms offered to consumers — delay in processing legitimate claims can create Magnuson-Moss exposure.
  - **FTC Warranty Rules (16 CFR Parts 700-703)** — FTC regulations implementing Magnuson-Moss: Pre-Sale Availability Rule (Part 702), Disclosure Rule (Part 701), Dispute Settlement Rule (Part 703). Relevant to how warranty terms are communicated to dealers and consumers.
  - **State Lemon Laws** — All 50 US states have lemon laws providing additional consumer remedies beyond Magnuson-Moss for vehicles with persistent defects. Some state lemon laws have specific time requirements for repair attempts (e.g., "reasonable number of repair attempts" or "30 calendar days out of service"). Warranty claim processing delays contribute to "days out of service" calculations.
  - **NHTSA TREAD Act (49 USC 30118-30120)** — Transportation Recall Enhancement, Accountability, and Documentation Act: requires OEMs to report early warning data (warranty claims, field reports, property damage/injury claims) to NHTSA for safety defect identification. Warranty claims data is a primary input to the TREAD early warning system. Claims that indicate potential safety defects must be flagged and reported — the warranty claim authorization process has a direct link to safety recall obligations.
  - **FTC Act Section 5 (15 USC 45)** — Prohibits unfair or deceptive trade practices. Systematic denial or delay of legitimate warranty claims could constitute an unfair trade practice. Several OEMs have faced FTC investigations for warranty claim practices.
  - **OEM Franchise/Dealer Agreements** — The OEM-dealer franchise agreement governs warranty claim submission requirements, reimbursement rates (parts markup, labor rates, sublet rates), claims submission deadlines (typically 30-90 days from repair completion), parts return requirements, and dealer audit/chargeback provisions. These are contractual, not regulatory, but they define the warranty claim workflow.
  - **ISO 10002:2018** — Quality Management — Customer Satisfaction — Guidelines for Complaints Handling in Organizations: provides guidance on the process of handling customer complaints, including warranty-related complaints. Applicable to the OEM's claim handling process design.
  - **IFRS 15 / ASC 606** — Revenue Recognition: warranty accruals and reserves are significant accounting items ($3B-$8B for major OEMs). Warranty claim processing affects reserve adequacy, and fraud-related claim leakage directly impacts warranty cost of sales (WCOS) and operating margin.
- Expert in **automotive warranty claim authorization workflows:**
  - **Standard OEM warranty claim flow:** (1) Customer brings vehicle to dealer with a complaint. (2) Dealer technician diagnoses the issue: reads DTCs (Diagnostic Trouble Codes) with OEM scan tool, follows OEM diagnostic procedures (service manual / TIS / OASIS), identifies the failed component. (3) For some repairs, dealer must obtain **prior authorization (PA)** from the OEM before proceeding — typically required for high-cost repairs (engine, transmission, hybrid battery, ADAS components) over a dollar threshold (e.g., $2,000-$5,000+). (4) Dealer performs the repair. (5) Dealer submits the warranty claim through the dealer portal (DealerConnect / GlobalConnect / TIS / OASIS / B2B Connect) with: repair order number, VIN, mileage, DTC codes, labor operation codes (OEM-specific flat-rate labor times), causal part number, parts replaced, sublet charges (if any). (6) **Claims Adjuster** reviews the claim: validates warranty coverage (is the vehicle within the warranty period? is the component covered under basic, powertrain, or extended warranty?), checks for TSBs (Technical Service Bulletins) or existing campaigns/recalls, verifies labor time against OEM flat-rate guide, reviews parts replaced against the causal complaint. (7) **Claim adjudication:** approve, deny (with reason code), or send back to dealer for correction (kick-back). (8) Approved claims generate a credit memo to the dealer (parts cost + labor reimbursement + sublet). (9) Parts Return Authorization (PRA) issued for causal parts — dealer must return specified parts to the OEM parts return center within 15-30 days. (10) Dealer payment processed (typically net 15-30 days).
  - **Prior Authorization (PA) workflow — the actual bottleneck.** For high-cost repairs, the dealer must obtain PA BEFORE performing the repair. The PA process typically involves: (a) Dealer contacts the PA team (phone or portal submission), (b) PA Claims Adjuster reviews the request — verifies coverage, reviews DTC codes, may request photos or additional diagnostic data, (c) PA authorized or denied, (d) Dealer performs the repair. The PA bottleneck is the real-world friction point — dealers waiting for PA approval while the customer's vehicle sits in the service bay. Standard PA turnaround is 2-4 hours for routine requests, 24-48 hours for complex requests requiring technical escalation.
  - **The "Warranty Director" as a single approval authority is a simplification.** In real OEMs, the claims authorization hierarchy is: (a) Auto-adjudication (system rules engine approves 60-80% of standard claims automatically — no human review), (b) Claims Adjuster (handles claims kicked out of auto-adjudication, typically $0-$2,000), (c) Senior Claims Analyst / Team Lead (approves claims above the adjuster's authority limit, typically $2,000-$10,000), (d) Warranty Manager / Regional Warranty Manager (approves claims $10,000-$50,000, goodwill/policy adjustments, and dealer disputes), (e) Warranty Director / VP of Warranty (approves claims above $50,000, fleet/recall-related claims, and systemic issue authorizations). The scenario's 1-of-1 from Warranty Director is only accurate for very high-value claims — most claims never reach the Warranty Director level.
  - **Fraud analytics in warranty claims.** OEM warranty fraud programs typically detect: (a) **Repeat VIN claims** — the same vehicle has an unusually high number of warranty claims (potential phantom repairs), (b) **Labor time inflation** — the dealer consistently claims more labor time than the OEM flat-rate guide allows, (c) **Parts harvesting** — the dealer replaces a part under warranty, keeps the new OEM part, and installs a used/aftermarket part on the customer's vehicle, (d) **Causal part substitution / shotgunning** — the dealer replaces multiple parts to mask the actual root cause (inflating parts cost), (e) **Out-of-warranty claims** — the dealer submits claims for vehicles that are past the warranty expiration date or mileage, (f) **Phantom repairs** — repairs that were never actually performed, (g) **Dealer network patterns** — unusually high CPV (claims per vehicle) or CPC (cost per claim) for a dealer compared to regional or national averages. Major OEMs lose $2B-$5B+ annually to warranty fraud and claim leakage. Fraud analytics platforms (Tavant warranty.ai, SAS Warranty Analytics, IBM Warranty Analytics) apply predictive models to flag suspicious claims before they are paid.
  - **The Regional Manager as a delegate is partially correct.** Regional Warranty Managers do exist and they do approve certain claim types (goodwill/policy adjustments, dealer disputes, regional campaign authorizations). However, their delegation authority is typically limited by dollar threshold, claim type, and dealer performance score. A Regional Manager typically CANNOT approve: (a) claims above their authority limit (e.g., $10,000-$25,000), (b) claims from dealers flagged by the SIU for audit, (c) claims involving potential safety defects (must be escalated to Technical Warranty / Engineering), (d) goodwill adjustments above a set dollar or percentage threshold.
  - **Missing: Warranty Management System (WMS) as a System actor.** The WMS is the system that processes claims, enforces auto-adjudication rules, routes claims requiring human review, manages dealer portals, and integrates with fraud analytics. It is analogous to the SLM/ERP System in supplier-cert, MES/ERP System in quality-inspection, and PLM/CMB System in joint-design.
  - **Missing: Fraud analytics engine / SIU.** The scenario description mentions "fraud analytics" and "claim leakage" but there is no actor representing the fraud detection system or the Special Investigations Unit.
  - **Missing: Technical Service Manager / Field Service Engineer.** For complex technical claims (engine/transmission failure, hybrid battery, ADAS), a technical specialist reviews the claim to verify the diagnosis, check for related TSBs, and confirm the repair procedure was correct. This is a common escalation path.
