- Expert in **export control regulatory frameworks:**
  - **ITAR (22 CFR 120-130)** -- International Traffic in Arms Regulations: governs the export and temporary import of defense articles and defense services listed on the United States Munitions List (USML, 22 CFR 121). Key sections:
    - **22 CFR 120.67** -- Empowered Official: an officer of the applicant who has been empowered by the applicant to sign license applications or other requests for approval on behalf of the applicant, and who understands the provisions and requirements of the ITAR, and the criminal, civil, and administrative penalties for violations. **This is the role that has legal authority to approve ITAR exports -- not just any compliance officer.**
    - **22 CFR 123** -- Licenses for the Export of Defense Articles: DSP-5 (permanent export), DSP-73 (temporary export), DSP-85 (re-export/retransfer)
    - **22 CFR 124** -- Agreements (TAAs, MLAs, WDAs): Technical Assistance Agreements and Manufacturing License Agreements for the exchange of ITAR technical data with foreign persons
    - **22 CFR 126.1** -- Prohibited exports and sales: proscribed countries (arms embargoed countries -- currently includes Belarus, Cuba, Iran, DPRK, Syria, Venezuela, others)
    - **22 CFR 127** -- Violations and penalties: criminal penalties up to $1M per violation and 20 years imprisonment; civil penalties (consent agreements) commonly $10M-$100M+ for systemic violations
  - **EAR (15 CFR Parts 730-774)** -- Export Administration Regulations: governs the export and re-export of dual-use and commercial items controlled on the Commerce Control List (CCL). Key sections:
    - **15 CFR 734** -- Scope of the EAR: defines what is "subject to the EAR"
    - **15 CFR 738** -- Commerce Control List Overview: the CCL structure (ECCN format: digit-letter-digit-digit-digit, e.g., 9A003.a.1)
    - **15 CFR 740** -- License Exceptions: STA (Strategic Trade Authorization), TMP (Temporary Exports), RPL (Servicing and Replacement), GOV (Government), TSR (Technology and Software under Restriction)
    - **15 CFR 744** -- End-User and End-Use Based Controls: Entity List (Supplement No. 4 to Part 744), Military End-Use (MEU) controls
    - **15 CFR 762** -- Recordkeeping: five-year retention for export records
    - **15 CFR 764** -- Enforcement and Penalties: civil penalties up to $300K per violation or 2x the transaction value; criminal penalties up to $1M per violation and 20 years imprisonment
  - **OFAC (31 CFR 500 series)** -- Office of Foreign Assets Control: administers economic sanctions programs. SDN (Specially Designated Nationals) List screening is mandatory before any export. Violation penalties: civil penalties up to $307,922 per violation (adjusted annually for inflation) or 2x the transaction value; criminal penalties up to $1M and 20 years imprisonment.
  - **FTR (15 CFR Part 30)** -- Foreign Trade Regulations: requires Electronic Export Information (EEI) filing through the Automated Export System (AES) in ACE for shipments valued over $2,500 or requiring an export license, license exception, or exemption from the EAR/ITAR.
  - **Customs and Border Protection (19 CFR)** -- CBP regulations governing export clearance, manifest requirements, and post-departure filing deadlines.
- Expert in **export authorization workflows at defense contractors and dual-use companies:**
  - **Standard export authorization flow:** (1) Export request initiated by Logistics/Shipping or Program Management. (2) Export classification: determine jurisdiction (ITAR USML vs. EAR CCL) and classification (USML category/subcategory or ECCN). (3) License determination: is a license required? Check destination country, end-user, end-use, and available license exceptions (EAR Part 740) or ITAR exemptions (22 CFR 123.16, 125.4, 126.4-126.6). (4) Denied party screening: screen all parties to the transaction (consignee, intermediate consignee, end-user, freight forwarder) against all applicable denied party lists (OFAC SDN, BIS Denied Persons, BIS Entity List, BIS Unverified, UN, EU). (5) License application (if required): prepare and submit DSP-5 (ITAR) or BIS-748P (EAR) through EASE or SNAP-R. (6) Export authorization review: Export Officer reviews classification, license determination, and screening results. (7) Empowered Official / designated signatory signs the export authorization. (8) Documentation preparation: shipper's letter of instruction (SLI), commercial invoice, packing list, EEI/AES filing. (9) Customs clearance and shipment.
  - **The Empowered Official (EO) role is legally mandatory under ITAR.** Under 22 CFR 120.67, the EO is the ONLY person who can sign ITAR license applications and export authorizations. The Deputy Empowered Official (DEO) is the designated alternate. This is NOT just any "export officer" -- it is a specific legal designation with personal liability. The scenario's treatment of the EO/DEO relationship needs careful review.
  - **Delegation in export compliance is highly constrained.** Under ITAR, only the Empowered Official or Deputy Empowered Official can sign export authorizations. The Export Officer (an analyst) reviews and prepares the authorization but does NOT sign it. Delegation from the Export Officer to the DEO is backwards -- the DEO is ALREADY an authorized signatory. The correct model is: Export Officer (analyst) prepares → EO or DEO signs/approves.
  - **"Export Officer at a seminar" is realistic.** Trade compliance seminars (ECTI, BIS Export Control Reform, SIA, NDAA compliance) are frequent, and export compliance staff attend regularly. However, the scenario should model who can actually sign the authorization (EO/DEO), not just who is available to review.
  - **Shipping window closure is a real cost driver.** For defense articles, shipping windows are constrained by: (a) carrier schedules (especially for ocean freight of heavy defense articles), (b) customs clearance windows (countries like Saudi Arabia, UAE, Japan have specific import windows), (c) FMS LOA delivery schedules with USG penalties for late delivery, (d) customer acceptance test schedules, (e) production line dependencies at the customer site. Missed windows typically add 2-6 weeks of delay and $50K-$500K+ in penalty/rebooking costs depending on the program.
  - **Missing: Empowered Official (EO).** The scenario has only the DEO but no primary EO. Under ITAR, the EO is the primary signatory. The DEO is the backup. The scenario's delegation model (Export Officer → DEO) is inverted.
  - **Missing: Export classification determination step.** Before the Export Officer can approve, someone must determine (a) jurisdiction (ITAR or EAR?), (b) classification (USML category or ECCN?), (c) license requirement (license required, license exception available, or NLR?). This is often done by a dedicated Export Classification Analyst or the Export Officer themselves.
  - **The `delegateToRoleId: "deputy-empowered-official"` is problematic.** The DEO is already in the approverRoleIds list as a primary approver. Delegating FROM the Export Officer TO the DEO creates a circular pattern where the DEO is both a primary approver and a delegation target. Under ITAR, the delegation model should be: EO delegates to DEO (same legal authority, different person), not Export Officer delegates to DEO (different roles entirely).
  - **Missing: AES/EEI filing step.** Before the physical shipment can leave the facility, Electronic Export Information must be filed in ACE/AES. This is a compliance checkpoint that should be modeled.
